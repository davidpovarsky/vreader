## Summary

Round-2 attempt at the deferred TTS-playback leg from
`feature-40-20260507.md` ("real TTS playback →
AVSpeechSynthesizer → coordinator → HighlightableTextView
rendering. Requires UI gesture path + audio session.").

Result: **inconclusive**. TTS audibly starts and the auto-scroll
companion feature (#41) clearly works (the view scrolls forward
through the fixture in real time). But across 5+ screenshots taken
at varying offsets during 0.6s–6s of playback at 1.2× speed, no
yellow sentence highlight is visible. Either the highlight isn't
rendering, or the simulator's audio path isn't firing
`willSpeakRangeOfSpeechString` reliably (so the coordinator never
gets a sentence offset to highlight), or the highlight redraws
faster than screenshot timing can catch.

Filing this as `partial`, **not** as a bug — the wiring is
present and round-1's 9-test slice still proves the coordinator's
internal logic. A follow-up round with verbose logging (or a
DebugBridge probe that exposes `uiState.highlightRange` mid-TTS)
is needed to discriminate between "highlight rendering broken" vs
"simulator audio session quirk".

## Observations

- **Auto-scroll (feature #41) clearly works.** Starting TTS at the top of
  CHAPTER I scrolled the view through CHAPTER II → CHAPTER III to "End of
  synthetic fixture excerpt." in roughly 4 seconds at 1.2× speed. The
  fixture is small (1708 bytes), so playback completes fast.
- **No yellow highlight visible** in any of the 5+ screenshots taken during
  active playback. The highlight color is `UIColor.systemYellow @ 40%
  alpha`, which on a white sepia/light background should produce a clearly
  visible pale yellow rect spanning a sentence — not a subtle effect.
- **Wiring is in place** (see code-read above): coordinator calls
  `uiState.highlightRange = range` in `updateHighlight`, the bridge
  observes the change at line 125 and re-sets the layout manager's
  `highlightRanges` at line 198, and `HighlightingLayoutManager.drawBackground`
  paints the yellow.
- **Two non-mutually-exclusive theories**:
  1. AVSpeechSynthesizer's `willSpeakRangeOfSpeechString` callback isn't
     firing reliably on the simulator (audio session not active or audio
     output muted). The coordinator's `updateHighlight(offset:)` would
     never be called → `uiState.highlightRange` stays nil → nothing
     drawn. But this contradicts the auto-scroll observation, since
     auto-scroll uses the same offset signal (`uiState.scrollToOffset` is
     set in the same call).
  2. The highlight IS being painted but for too brief a window (sub-frame)
     because `currentOffsetUTF16` updates very rapidly (per word, not per
     sentence). If the layout manager redraws on every word boundary, the
     net effect could be a flicker invisible to a screenshot. This would
     also explain why auto-scroll feels smooth but highlight feels
     missing — auto-scroll position is monotone (always advancing), so
     visually-stable; the highlight sentence range could be shifting
     forward sub-frame.
- **Recommended follow-up**: add a DebugBridge probe that exposes
  `uiState.highlightRange` and `uiState.highlightIsTemporary` over
  `vreader-debug://eval?bridge=text&js=...` (or the Swift equivalent).
  Then a verify round can poll the value during TTS playback and confirm
  whether the range is being set at the rate of word boundaries (theory 2)
  or never set at all (theory 1). Without this probe, on-simulator visual
  verification of the highlight is unreliable.

`TTSHighlightCoordinator`'s internal correctness. The
AVSpeechSynthesizer ↔ coordinator ↔ `HighlightableTextView` end-to-end
visual leg is **deferred** pending a discriminating signal (debug probe
or device verification with reliable audio output). Feature row stays at
`DONE`; do not flip to `VERIFIED` until either:

1. A debug probe shows `uiState.highlightRange` cycling through
   sentence ranges during TTS playback (proving theory 2 — paint-flicker
   masks visibility on screenshots), OR
2. A real-device verify round captures a screenshot with the visible
   yellow background (proving the simulator audio quirk is the limit).
