The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog near the riverbank at dawn. Mist rose from the water as the first light touched the reeds, and a heron stood motionless among them, patient as stone.

Far off, a bell rang twice and then was still. The path along the bank was soft with fallen leaves, and the air smelled of cold earth and woodsmoke drifting from a chimney somewhere beyond the trees.

She walked without hurry, counting the herons she had seen that week, naming each bend of the river as her grandmother once had. The words felt old in her mouth, worn smooth like the river stones she gathered as a child and kept in a tin beneath her bed.

By the time the sun cleared the far hills the mist had thinned to nothing, and the whole valley lay open and bright and ordinary, as if the strange grey hour before had never happened at all.
